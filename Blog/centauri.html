Alpha centauri

on 1 May 2023


Our stellar neighbours have a surprising lot in common with our sun, and yet are so different. The Alpha Centauri System consists of Three different stars, unlike our solar system with only one. The system consists of two sun-like stars Alpha Centauri A and Alpha centauri B (With the nice names of Rigil Kentaurus and Toliman respectively) With a small, red dwarf star (Alpha Centarui C) orbiting around both, Called Proxima Centauri.

Alpha centauri A and B

And these two larger stars are really similar to the sun, at 1.1 and 0.9 Solar masses. The two larger stars are close enough that they appear to the naked eye as a single star, becoming the third brightest star in the night sky (The two stars are as close to each other as saturn is to the sun!). Also, because they are so similar and are close enough, Both stars form a binary system, where the stars orbit a Barycentre, A common point in space. (Side note: Our solar system can actually be considered a binary system. Jupiter is massive enough to make the sun 'wobble' a bit, and when jupiter and saturn are on the same side of the sun, the solar system's barycentre moves from inside the sun to outside.)

Proxima Centauri

Proxima Centauri is a small red dwarf star so faint its not visible to the naked eye. The small star orbits around the two larger stars at a distance of 0.21 Light years away, far enough that it is actually the Closest star to the sun, at 4.24 Light years.

The planets of the system

Only the small proxima centauri has any confirmed planets around it, Proxima B - an earth sized planet in the habitable zone- and Proxima D - A candidate Sub-Earth.

The controveries of the planets

Including all the controveries, The three stars combined have 5 planets, with three orbiting the small proxima centauri.

There's an unconfirmed planet (C1, Or candidate 1) around the largest star in the system, and while we don't have any details, its year is almost as long as ours(if it exists - it could just be some dust). When it comes to the other star, there's a planet that was confirmed, but later disproven. On top of this, another planet was theorized based on a transit event, Where the star suddenly dimmed and re-brightened, but a planet would cause regular dimmings, and another dimming event was not observed.

When it comes to Proxima C, it is thought to orbit only slightly further out than earth, but one year would still be 1,900 days long. Its about 7 times more massive than Earth, and it could have a huge ring system like saturn. Another paper disputes its existence though.

Of course, there very well be more planets waiting to be discovered. Current estimates place the probability of finding an Earth-like planet around Alpha Centauri at 75%. There also may exist planets orbiting around both stars as a whole, far from the stars themselves - Like the theorized planet 9. Given how similar the system is to ours, It is highly likely for more similarities to be uncovered soon.
